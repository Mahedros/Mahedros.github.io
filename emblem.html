There are many Fire Emblem games, and some are better than others. As an expert in this series, it falls to me to tell you the completely objective truth about which games are good and which games are bad.

Anyone who tells you a different ranking is wrong, and cannot be trusted.

Click an entry to read my extended thoughts on a game.

The Ranking

  1. Radiant Dawn (FE 10, Wii, 2007)

    This game has everything I want. A strong narrative, interesting characters, and great gameplay that does things nothing else in the series does. Switching between armies forces you to use a variety of units (and lets you do some clever things when you know which units move between armies), the skill system is great (even if the mastery skills need a re-balance), ledges are a great mechanic that don't exist in any other game, and the endgame really delivers on tying everything together.

    I do wish laguz felt less gimmicky and more usable outside of the royals.

  2. Three Houses (FE 16, Switch, 2019)

    I'm not getting involved in "the discourse". Yes, there is an objectively correct answer to which route is the best, but I'm not going to say it.

    Battalions are a great system, and I love the way the boss monsters encourage you to make use of them. I also really like the way the magic system encourages you to not horde all your powerful spells until the game is over. The monastery system gives good opportunities to make characters feel less like a generic blob of combat stats, and the smaller roster size lets characters shine. It's also the only game with actual new game plus features that make subsequent playthroughs feel significantly different.

    The routes do have a bit too much overlap for my liking, and the one truly unique route does sometimes feel like it didn't get as much attention as the other three.

  3. Fates: Conquest (FE 14, 3DS, 2015)

    Conquest really delivers on challenge in a way that just raising the difficulty setting often doesn't. Its base difficulty setting manages to present a challenge without resorting to giving enemies absurd garbage the way Awakening Lunatic or FE 12 Lunatic Reverse mode do. This game manages to strike a great balance between the cookie-cutter feeling of Birthright and the "throw weird ideas at the wall" feeling of Revelation.

    I'm not going to pretend the narrative for Conquest really delievered on the concept it was trying for. It doesn't. Garon is cartoonishly evil and it's really hard to feel like you're the good guy while siding with him. The child units also feel incredibly shoehorned in to capitalize on their success in Awakening, and they no longer have Awakening's time travel plot to make them feel justified (I won't repeat myself later, but this is also an issue for Birthright and Revelation).

  4. Path of Radiance (FE 9, GameCube, 2005)

    This sets the stage for Radiant Dawn in an excellent way, but the challenge level just isn't there. It's a great game with excellent gameplay and a strong story, it just needs some refinement.

  5. Engage (FE 17, Switch, 2023)

    This is a game of extremes. The mechanics are excellent, maybe even the best in the entire series, but the narrative is weak even by FE standards. The amount of options you have to customize your units is great, but the frequency with which units are introduced only to have another unit that does their job better immediately replace them is frustrating.

    The game is also screaming out for a new game plus to make experimenting with all the customization options more feasible, but it doesn't have one. By the time you unlock a lot of the options, the game is over.

    I really want to love this game, but it often feels like the game is actively trying to discourage that love.

  6. The Binding Blade (FE 6, GBA, 2002)

    A better challenge than the other GBA games? A secret ending with sane unlock conditions (FE 7 should have taken notes)? Our boy Roy? This game is fantastic; it just needs some small tweaks to really shine.

    • Let Roy promote earlier.
    • Let axes be able to hit the broadside of a barn.
    • Don't have thrones grant +30 avoid to bosses.
    • Don't combine sand, fog of war, and a forcibly deployed underleveled unit in 1 map.
    • Don't have your secret ending unlock condition contribute to the instinct to hoard strong equipment.
    • Let thieves promote.
    • Add a light magic user that isn't a healer.

    But ultimately, these nitpicky things are worth it. Binding Blade is great.

  7. The Sacred Stones (FE 8, GBA, 2004)

    This holds a special place in my heart as my first FE game. The world map and the ability to grind delieverd on some of FE 2's weird ideas and mixed up the formula, letting you try units you might not otherwise use. That's nice.

    It is a bit easy, and the main story is a bit short. At the end of the day, I just end up wishing the game had more in it.

  8. Fates: Revelation (FE 14, 3DS, 2015)

    Conquest was challenging mechanically interesting in a good way. Revelation is here to teach us the valuable lesson that interesting things don't always work out. "Unique things can still be bad" will be a rercurring point in these rankings.

    Some of the map mechanics here are truly unique and I applaud the risk taking this game commits to. Unfortunately, shoveling snow is not a good Fire Emblem mechanic, and I wish that was an exaggeration or a joke. Also, having a golden route where everyone gets along is nice on paper, but it presents some game balance issues that just go unaddressed. Xander and Ryoma are both incredible units in Conquest and Birthright. Having both of them at the same time is a bit much.

  9. Awakening (FE 13, 3DS, 2012)

    Awakening did some great stuff that laid the foundations for the more refined versions in Fates. Pair up is great, but it's a bit too good and enemies never use it. The skill system is great, but without personal skills builds can get a bit samey.

    The narrative is fine. Time travel plots are usually red flags, so I can't complain too much. The Valm arc does feel like filler though.

  10. Fates: Birthright (FE 14, 3DS, 2015)

    Birthright often feels like the "bare minimum" game of the Fates trio. It's remarkably easy, it has a very cookie-cutter narrative, and just doesn't take any risks. It's enjoyable, but to go high on the ranking it's not enough to just avoid mistakes.

  11. The Blazing Blade (FE 7, GBA, 2003)

    The first FE ever to make it to the West (just titled "Fire Emblem" here for some reason), this is a lot of people's first FE. It's most notable for confusing Melee fans by not featuring Marth or Roy.

    Jokes aside, this a pretty bog standard FE game. It doesn't do much mechanically that stands out, and while the narrative manages to avoid the usual "evil empire invades your country and the prince goes into exile" it's still not a remarkable story either.

    What really hurts the story here is that a lot of the most interesting narrative beats are locked behind bonus chapters with incredibly unintuitive unlock conditions, including one that is a bonus chapter unlocked from within another bonus chapter and only available on a second playthrough.

  12. Echoes: Shadows of Valentia (FE 15, 3DS, 2017, Remake of FE 2)

    FE 2 did a lot of unique things, and Shadows of Valentia is a pretty faithful remake. Unfortunately, it also has some of the worst map design in the series. Expect lots of open fields where your army and the opposing army just charge at each other, which is just uninteresting. The desert map is bad by desert map standards, you have infinite range teleporting enemies that can one-round weaker units, enemies with map-wide AoE attacks, and a boss who can only take damage every 4th time you get in a fight with him.

    Remember friends, unique things can still be bad.

  13. New Mystery of the Emblem - Heroes of Light and Shadow (FE 12, DS, 2010, Remake of FE 3)

    Some of the narrative things this adds on to FE 3 can be a bit weird, but it's a solid game. It does suffer a bit from the DS as a platform, as some enemies have such large movement ranges that they can reach you from off screen, which makes seeing the bigger picture of the map a bit annoying. People who dislike avatar characters will get mad about Kris, but remember: they're wrong.

  14. Shadow Dragon (FE 11, DS, 2008, Remake of FE 1)

    This is fine. Nothing special, a bit ugly, and kinda underwhelming if you paid full price and expected something more than a fresh coat of paint on an old game, but it's fine. It plays like a modern game for the most part. Only 3 real major issues:

    1. It introduced reclassing into the series, which ruins the unique identities of units
    2. It nerfed endgame FE 1 Marth from an unstoppable monster into one of the worse lords in the series
    3. It gates chapters behind getting your units killed

    After this point, we cross into "I don't recommend this" territory. You've been warned.

  15. Shadow Dragon and the Blade of Light (FE 1, NES, 1990)

    There are a number of quality of life features the modern player will quickly notice are missing here, but one dwarfs the rest in its importance. A good tactics game should give you the basic information you need in order to be make plans that have a chance to be successful. It modern FE games, this takes the form of visible movement ranges and data on hit rates, dodge rates, damage output, attack speed, etc. that allow you to have an idea of how a fight will go.

    This game does not give you that information. You get your unit's raw stats, the enemy unit's raw stats, and that's it. I'd say you have to figure it out on your own, but the game doesn't even give you the formulas used to calculate anything. It doesn't give you weapon stats. It's all just guesswork, googling, and memorization.

  16. Genealogy of the Holy War (FE 4, SNES, 1996)

    Remember how I said not to trust anyone who disagrees with these rankings? That's especially important for this game. Some people will tell you this is the best FE game, with a fantastic narrative and gameplay unlike anything else in the series.

    These people are not your friends. Say it with me once again: Unique things can still be bad.

    The terrible unit balancing, the obnoxiously large maps, and the atrocious inventory system are not good things, and the rumored remake would do well to adjust them.

    At the end of the day, if all a game can offer me is a good narrative, I'm better off just reading a book.

Fire Emblem Games that aren't Fire Emblem Games

  1. Warriors: Three Hopes (Switch, 2022)

    This refined the ideas in the first FE Warriors game quite nicely. The narrative kinda pissed me off in the way it treated certain characters, but I have to be consistent in placing greater emphasis on mechanics than narrative.

  2. Warriors (3DS & Switch, 2017)

    A bit more moveset variety would have been nice, but as a fan of musou games this was everything I wanted. Yeah the narrative was pretty bad, but it's a musou game. It's not about story, it's about cutting your way through big groups of enemies.

  3. Heroes (iOS / Android, 2017)

    It's a gacha game with absurd levels of power creep. I uninstalled it a while ago and never looked back.
